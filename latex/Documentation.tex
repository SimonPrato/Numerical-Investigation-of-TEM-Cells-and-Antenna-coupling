\noindent
{\bf Abstract}} \\
\vspace*{0.3 cm}

Electrically small conducting structures carrying high-frequency currents and voltages can generate substantial electromagnetic emissions, leading to challenges regarding the electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) of electronic devices. Measurement of these emissions in a transverse electromagnetic (TEM) cell is a widely adopted and standardized procedure. In this thesis, a theoretical framework based on the Lorentz reciprocity theorem and modal field expansion is developed to relate the electric and magnetic dipole moments of electrically small antennas to the output port signals of the TEM cell. The coupling is described by equivalent circuit models for both capacitive and inductive antenna types, and the entire framework is validated through finite element method simulations using Ansys HFSS.

Particular emphasis is placed on electric and magnetic dipole moments as effective characterizations of electrically small radiating sources. In this framework, the electric dipole moment is associated with displacement currents coupling to the TEM cell septum, while the magnetic dipole moment is related to the voltage induced on it. The capacitive or inductive character of the antenna emerges as the primary determinant of the resulting dipole moments and their frequency dependence. These relationships are investigated for monopole, loop, gapped-loop, and inverted-F antenna geometries. Furthermore, the influence of higher-order propagating modes on the coupling behavior is examined, demonstrating that mode alignment between the antenna orientation and the modal field distribution governs power transfer to the output ports.

Additionally, the shielding effectiveness of materials in the presence of electrically small antennas is numerically investigated in Ansys HFSS using the ASTM ES7-83 method and a dual TEM cell, allowing separate determination of electric and magnetic shielding effectiveness. It is demonstrated that the equivalent dipole moment representation assists in this investigation, as the electric and magnetic dipole moments directly identify which shielding material properties are relevant for a given source. The results are directly applicable in the design of electronic systems with EMC constraints, where the established theoretical framework assists in the selection of appropriate shielding materials.
