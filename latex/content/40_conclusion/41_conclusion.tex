This thesis presents investigations of electrically small antennas and their coupling with a TEM cell using the finite element method. It further discusses applications of the framework created.

In this thesis, dipole moments equivalent to the electrically small antennas are calculated, whose magnitudes directly correlate with the electric and magnetic coupling of the antenna with the TEM cell. It finds, that the electric dipole moment correlates directly to the displacement current towards the septum, and the magnetic dipole moment to the voltage induced on the septum. An equivalent circuit model, both for capacitive and inductive antennas coupling to the TEM cell, is developed. 

The relation of different geometrical and electrical antenna parameters to the equivalent dipole moments is investigated. An increase in Q-factor or decrease in resonance frequency of the antenna has been found to increase non-linear dipole moments frequency-behavior. The electric dipole moment generated by an antenna increases primarily with its physical height, due to increased displacement currents toward the septum. The magnetic dipole moment increases with the loop area normal to the magnetic field intensity of a propagating mode in the TEM cell. If the loop is not closed, a magnetic dipole moment can still exist due to curling electric field intensities $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} \neq 0$ forming perpendicular to the magnetic field intensity. 

Further research could involve the measurement of such antennas with a real TEM cell, or the numerical analysis with other waveguides, such as the IC stripline. The framework in this thesis could be used to increase EMC of electronic systems containing electrically small, radiating structures, or represent them with dipole moments for less computational effort in complex simulation models.

%\section*{Summary of Key Findings}
%
%The theoretical foundation established in \cref{sec:dipole-theory,sec:guided-waves} connects the electric and magnetic dipole moments of electrically small antennas directly to measurable TEM cell quantities. The electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ was found to correlate with the displacement current flowing toward the septum, while the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ correlates with the voltage induced across the septum. These relationships were formalized through the Green's function framework for guided waves and subsequently validated numerically across five distinct antenna geometries.
%
%The monopole antenna, as the canonical capacitive antenna, generates a predominantly electric dipole moment that increases linearly with frequency. Its feedpoint voltage remains constant across the investigated frequency range, confirming the absence of inductive coupling. An equivalent RLC circuit model was developed to represent the coupling between the monopole antenna and the TEM cell, from which the dipole moments can be directly extracted via Kirchhoff's laws. The coupling capacitance $C_k \approx 1.68\,\mathrm{pF}$ was identified as the primary link between the antenna and the TEM cell, while the mutual inductances were found to be negligible. The output power predicted by the equivalent dipole model agreed with the full numerical simulation within $\pm 10\,\mathrm{\mu W}$.
%
%The loop antenna, as the canonical inductive antenna, primarily generates a magnetic dipole moment that dominates at low frequencies. Unlike the monopole, the loop antenna exhibits non-linear dipole moment behavior over frequency, a consequence of the antenna's own frequency-dependent voltage and current characteristics rather than transitions between near-field and far-field coupling regimes. A dedicated numerical study using two TEM cells of significantly different sizes confirmed that the $k \cdot r$ product remains well below unity throughout the investigated frequency range, establishing that coupling operates entirely within the near-field regime and that field-region transitions do not contribute to the observed non-linearity. The loop antenna was further shown to inevitably produce a secondary electric dipole moment at higher frequencies, arising from displacement currents driven by the increasing antenna potential. The equivalent circuit model for the loop antenna successfully reproduced both dipole moments, with greatest accuracy at higher frequencies.
%
%Three additional antenna structures — the loop antenna with a gap, the inverted-F antenna, and the center-fed monopole — were analyzed as geometrical variants of the two reference cases. The key finding is that the reactive character of the antenna, whether dominantly capacitive or inductive, rather than the specific geometric shape, is the primary determinant of the equivalent dipole moments and their frequency dependence. Introducing a gap into the return path of a loop antenna shifts it toward capacitive behavior, reducing the magnetic dipole moment while enhancing the electric one, with the degree of this shift governed by the gap height. Both the IFA and CFM, despite including a loop of identical area to the reference loop antenna, exhibited a reduced $\mathbf{m}_m$ and an enhanced $\mathbf{m}_e$ due to the additional linear antenna arms, which redirect current away from the loop and increase charge-driven displacement currents toward the septum.
%
%The electric dipole moment generated by any antenna was found to increase primarily with the physical height of the conducting structure, owing to the resulting increase in displacement currents toward the septum. The magnetic dipole moment increases with the loop area normal to the magnetic field component of the propagating mode. In antennas with an interrupted return path, a magnetic dipole moment can persist despite the absence of a closed conductive loop, arising from rotational electric field intensities $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} \neq 0$ that produce an effective magnetic source even without net conductive current encircling an area.
%
%The shielding investigations demonstrated that a material's wave impedance relative to the source near-field impedance determines its shielding effectiveness. Barium titanate, with its high relative permittivity and low wave impedance, exhibited good electric shielding effectiveness but poor or negative magnetic shielding effectiveness, as its impedance is well matched to magnetic near-field sources. Ferrite showed the complementary behavior: effective magnetic shielding but poor electric shielding, owing to its elevated material wave impedance from the high relative permeability. Silver proved the most versatile shielding material, exhibiting the highest overall shielding effectiveness across both field polarizations. These near-field shielding results were shown to be consistent with the far-field ASTM ES7-83 measurements performed in a single TEM cell, and were further validated by enclosure experiments on the monopole and loop antennas. The monopole antenna's radiation was effectively suppressed by barium titanate, while the loop antenna's radiation was suppressed by ferrite, confirming the direct connection between an antenna's dominant near-field character and the appropriate shielding material.
%
%Replacing the full antenna models with their equivalent dipole moments inside shielding enclosures yielded good agreement in cases where the shielding material is not well matched to the antenna's dominant near-field type. When the material is a highly effective shield against the antenna's primary radiation mechanism, the accuracy of the dipole-moment substitution decreases, indicating that the interaction between the source and the enclosure modifies the effective dipole moments.

%\section*{Outlook}
%
%Several directions for future work emerge from the results of this thesis. The most direct extension is an experimental validation of the presented simulation models using a physical TEM cell. Measurement uncertainties inherent to the real setup would provide insight into the robustness of the equivalent dipole models and the accuracy of the extracted lumped circuit parameters. The numerical framework presented here could also be extended to other waveguide environments, such as the IC stripline, which is widely used in standardized immunity testing of integrated circuits.
%
%The equivalent circuit models developed in this thesis provide a compact and computationally efficient representation of electrically small radiating structures, and could be integrated directly into system-level simulation tools to predict EMC behavior of complex electronic assemblies without the need for full-wave simulation of every individual component. In this context, the observation that the dipole moments require adjustment when the antenna is placed inside a closely fitted shielding enclosure points to the need for enclosure-aware correction factors in such models.
%
%From a shielding design perspective, the finding that each material type preferentially attenuates either the electric or magnetic near-field component suggests that composite or layered shielding structures, combining materials with complementary impedance characteristics, could achieve superior broadband performance. Finally, investigation of rotating or obliquely oriented dipole moments and crossed-dipole excitations within TEM cells would extend the present results to the general case of EUT models whose equivalent sources cannot be reduced to a single electric or magnetic dipole.
