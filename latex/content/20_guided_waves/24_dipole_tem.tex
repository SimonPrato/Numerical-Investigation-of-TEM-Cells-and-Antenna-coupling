\subsection{Radiating Sources in TEM Cells}\label{sec:rad_source_tem}

Applying the Lorentz reciprocity theorem and the modal field distributions 
derived in the preceding sections, the coupling of an arbitrary current 
source to each propagating mode of the TEM cell is determined. This result 
is then specialized to electric and magnetic dipole moment sources, and subsequently to the electrically small antennas they represent.

\subsubsection{Arbitrary source}

Consider a current density $\mathbf{J}_1$ exciting the TEM cell, as shown in 
\autoref{fig:reciprocitytemcell}. In general, such a current requires external fields to sustain it. However, since the purpose of this analysis is to determine the fields radiated by $\mathbf{J}_1$, these external fields are excluded, following the analytical setup of~\cite[p.~360]{Collin_2015}. Only the radiated fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ are considered, which satisfy Maxwell's equations~\cite[p.~360]{Collin_2015}:

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		\nabla\times\mathbf{H}=\mathbf{J}_1 + j\omega\epsilon_0\mathbf{E}.
	\end{equation}	
	\begin{equation}
		\nabla \times \mathbf{E}=-j\omega\mu_0\mathbf{H},
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}


\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{content/img/reciprocity_tem_cell}
	\caption{TEM cell with an arbitrary current source $\mathbf{J}_1$ along the line $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ \cite{Collin_2015}. $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ are the field intensities induced by the current. $\mathbf{e}^+_n$ and $\mathbf{e}^-_n$ are outgoing fields towards both output ports of the TEM cell. $S_1$ and $S_2$ denote the surfaces at the output ports, and $d\mathbf{s}_1'$, $d\mathbf{s}_2'$ the corresponding outward-directed vector surface elements.}
	\label{fig:reciprocitytemcell}
\end{figure}

Applying the integral form of the Lorentz reciprocity theorem in \eqref{eqn:lorentz_rec_theorem_int}, where its other source terms are neglected $\mathbf{J}_2=\mathbf{M}_1=\mathbf{M}_2=0$, yields

\begin{equation}
	\oiint _S (\mathbf{E}_n^\pm\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^\pm)\,d\mathbf{s'}=\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm\,dv'.
	\label{eqn:J1_propagating_waves}
\end{equation}

The integration is carried out over the volume $V$ enclosed by the TEM cell walls, the septum, and the output port surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$. The Lorentz reciprocity theorem therefore establishes a relation between the fields $\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{H}$ generated by the source $\mathbf{J}_1$ and the fields $\mathbf{E}_n^\pm$, $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ of an arbitrary mode $n$, which will be used extensively throughout this thesis. Since the tangential fields vanish at the perfectly conducting walls and septum, the only non-zero contributions arise from the output port surfaces, yielding

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		\iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times 
		\mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' = 
		\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+\,dv',
		\label{eqn:s1_lorentz}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\iint_{S_2} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^-\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times 
		\mathbf{H}_n^-\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' = 
		\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-\,dv'.
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

The following analysis focuses on the surface $S_1$ and its associated integral 
equation~\eqref{eqn:s1_lorentz}, as the treatment of $S_2$ follows analogously. 
Substituting the modal expansions \eqref{eqn:a_modal_superposition1}--\eqref{eqn:b_modal_superposition2} 
for the fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ radiated by $\mathbf{J}_1$ into the 
left-hand side yields

\begin{align}
	\iint_{S_1} &\left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)
	\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \sum_m a_m\mathbf{H}_m^+
	-\sum_m a_m\mathbf{E}_m^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \sum_m a_m \iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_m^+
	-\mathbf{E}_m^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}'.
\end{align}

Applying the orthogonality condition~\eqref{eqn:norm_power} and the normalization 
condition~\eqref{eqn:unit_power}, the coefficient $a_n$ of mode $n$ can be isolated as

\begin{align}
	\iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}
	-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}'
	&= a_n \iint_{S_1} \left(-\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+
	-\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' 
	= -2a_n,
\end{align}
leading to
\begin{equation}
	a_n = -\frac{1}{2}\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+\,dv',
	\label{eqn:an_ej}
\end{equation}
and analogously
\begin{equation}
	b_n = -\frac{1}{2}\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-\,dv'.
	\label{eqn:bn_ej}
\end{equation}
This result is significant, as it directly relates an arbitrary source $\mathbf{J}_1$ to the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ of every propagating mode in the TEM cell. Consequently, knowledge of the normalized field distributions of the relevant modes over a frequency range of interest is sufficient to fully characterize the output fields generated by any internal source.

Furthermore, the output power at each port can be directly expressed in terms of the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$. For the TEM mode, this takes a particularly simple 
form, hence it is presented here as an example. Combining the normalization condition~\eqref{eqn:unit_power} with the modal fields 
$\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$ and $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$ and their respective 
coefficients $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $b_\mathrm{TEM}$ yields
\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:power_of_poynting}
	\begin{align}
		P_{\mathrm{out,1}} &= \iint_{S_1} \mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}\,d\mathbf{s}'\nonumber\\ 
		&= \iint_{S_1} \frac{1}{2}\,\Re\left\{ 
		\left(a_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^+\right) \times 
		\left(a_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^+\right)^* 
		\right\}\,d\mathbf{s}'= \frac{|a_\mathrm{TEM}|^2}{2},
		\label{eqn:power_of_poynting1}
	\end{align}
	\begin{align}
		P_{\mathrm{out,2}} &= \iint_{S_2} \mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
		&= \iint_{S_2} \frac{1}{2}\,\Re\left\{ 
		\left(b_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^-\right) \times 
		\left(b_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^-\right)^* 
		\right\}\,d\mathbf{s}' = \frac{|b_\mathrm{TEM}|^2}{2},
		\label{eqn:power_of_poynting2}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where the time-averaged Poynting vector $\mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}$ of the TEM mode is 
purely real, such that
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av} 
	= \frac{1}{2}\Re\left\{\mathbf{E}
	\times\mathbf{H}^*\right\}
	= \frac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}.
	\label{eqn:equivalent_tem}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Equivalent dipole moments}\label{sec:equ-dip-mom}

Since this thesis focuses on dipole moment sources in the TEM cell, the general source formulation from the previous section is now applied to the dipole moment case. Substituting the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e = I_0 \,\mathbf{d}$, defined in \eqref{eqn:m_e_def}, as the source current $\mathbf{J}_1$ integrated over the volume $V$ into the Lorentz reciprocity theorem \eqref{eqn:an_ej} and \eqref{eqn:bn_ej} gives~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:an_bn_me}
	\begin{equation}
		a_n = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+,
		\label{eqn:an_me}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		b_n = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-.
		\label{eqn:bn_me}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

These two expressions are compactly written as

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm,
	\label{eqn:dipole_tem_waves}
\end{equation}

where the upper and lower entries correspond to the `$+$' and `$-$' cases 
of \eqref{eqn:an_bn_me}, respectively.

The general source formulation is now specialized to the case of a magnetic dipole 
moment $\mathbf{M}_m$, following the approach of Collin~\cite[p.~361]{Collin_2015}. 
The source current density $\mathbf{J}_1$ is modeled as a constant current $I_0$ flowing 
around an infinitesimally small loop along a curve $C$, which gives

\begin{align}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} 
	&= -\frac{I_0}{2}\oint_C \mathbf{E}_n^\pm \,d\mathbf{l} \nonumber \\
	&= -\frac{I_0}{2}\iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf{E}_n^\pm 
	\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \frac{j\omega\mu_0 I_0}{2}\iint_{S} \mathbf{H}_n^\pm 
	\,d\mathbf{s}'.
	\label{eqn:mag_dipole_moment_tem}
\end{align}

The surface integral is thus expressed in terms of the normalized magnetic field
distribution $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ of mode $n$. To simplify this integral, the magnetic
dipole moment $\mathbf{M}_m$ with units of
$\mathrm{A\,m^2}$ is introduced, defined as an electric current $I_0$
flowing in a loop of vector area $\mathbf{A}$~\cite[p.~361]{Collin_2015}:

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{M}_m = I_0\,\mathbf{A}.
	\label{eqn:M_m}
\end{equation}

Since the loop is assumed to be
infinitesimally small, $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ is treated as constant over the loop area,
allowing the substitution of $\mathbf{M}_m = I_0\,\mathbf{A}$
into~\eqref{eqn:mag_dipole_moment_tem}, which yields

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix}
	= \frac{j\omega\mu_0}{2}\,\mathbf{M}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm.
\end{equation}

As discussed in \cref{sec:mag_dip}, the magnetic dipole moment is conventionally
expressed in numerical codes and in the literature via a magnetic current flowing
along a given length, defined as $\mathbf{m}_m = I_m\mathbf{L}$
in~\eqref{eqn:mag_dipole_moment}. To rewrite the above result in terms of
$\mathbf{m}_m$, the equivalence between the magnetic dipole moment expressed as an
electric current loop $\mathbf{M}_m$ and as a magnetic current element $I_m\mathbf{L}$
is employed. These two representations produce identical electric and magnetic field
distributions, and are related by~\cite[p.~237]{Balanis_1997}

\begin{equation}
	I_m \mathbf{L} = j\omega\mu_0 \mathbf{A} I_0.
	\label{eqn:magn_current_curr_loop}
\end{equation}

Inserting the equivalence
$\mathbf{m}_m = \mu_0\omega\,\mathbf{M}_m$~\cite{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024}
into the preceding expression then yields the compact result

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = \frac{j}{2}\,\mathbf{m}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm.
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments_mag}
\end{equation}

In the presence of both an electric and a magnetic dipole moment, their contributions superpose, giving \cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}
	\left(-\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm 
	+ j\,\mathbf{m}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm\right).
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments}
\end{equation}

This expression relates the dipole moments $\mathbf{m}_e$ and $\mathbf{m}_m$ directly 
to the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ of any propagating mode $n$. Since the TEM mode 
is of large interest in this thesis, \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments} is now specialized 
using the relation between the normalized TEM mode fields~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981},

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm = \pm\,\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z\times\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm / \eta_0.
	\label{eqn:he_wave}
\end{equation}

Substituting this into \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments} yields~\cite{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_\mathrm{TEM} \\ b_\mathrm{TEM}\end{pmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm\cdot
	\left(\mathbf{m}_e 
	\pm j\frac{\mathbf{m}_m\times\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z}{\eta_0}\right),
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments_tem}
\end{equation}

where the $\pm$ sign on the magnetic term reflects the anti-symmetry of the TEM 
magnetic field at the two output ports, $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^+ = 
-\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^-$, as shown in \autoref{fig:reciprocitytemcelltemmode}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{content/img/reciprocity_tem_cell_tem_mode}
	\caption{Field distribution of the TEM mode, highlighting the anti-phase magnetic 
		fields at the two output ports.}
	\label{fig:reciprocitytemcelltemmode}
\end{figure}

Since \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments_tem} requires knowledge of only $\mathbf{E}^\pm_\mathrm{TEM}$, 
it is particularly convenient for determining the dipole moments from measured or 
simulated port data. The individual contributions of $\mathbf{m}_e$ and $\mathbf{m}_m$ 
can be separated by forming the sum and difference of $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and 
$b_\mathrm{TEM}$~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981},

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:abm}
	\begin{equation}
		a_\mathrm{TEM} + b_\mathrm{TEM} = 
		-\mathbf{m}_{e}\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm,
		\label{eqn:ifa_me}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		a_\mathrm{TEM} - b_\mathrm{TEM} = 
		-j\frac{\left(\mathbf{m}_{m}\times\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z\right)
			\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}{\eta_0},
		\label{eqn:ifa_mm}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where the dipole moments are assumed to be located at the midpoint ($z=0$) between the two 
output ports. A displacement 
along the $z$-direction would introduce a phase shift between 
$\mathbf{E}^+_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $\mathbf{E}^-_\mathrm{TEM}$, which would invalidate 
the separation in \eqref{eqn:abm}.

The expressions in \eqref{eqn:abm} yield useful physical insight into how electric and magnetic dipole moments couple to the TEM cell. Consider first a pure electric dipole moment, $\mathbf{m}_m = \mathbf{0}$. The right-hand side of \eqref{eqn:ifa_mm} vanishes, giving $a_\mathrm{TEM} = b_\mathrm{TEM}$: the two output port signals are identical and therefore in phase. Conversely, for a pure magnetic dipole moment, $\mathbf{m}_e = \mathbf{0}$, the right-hand side of \eqref{eqn:ifa_me} vanishes, giving $a_\mathrm{TEM} = -b_\mathrm{TEM}$: the two output port signals are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign, corresponding to a phase shift of $\pi$. In general, the phase relationship between $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $b_\mathrm{TEM}$ therefore directly identifies the nature of the dominant source. In-phase outputs indicate a predominantly electric dipole character, while out-of-phase outputs indicate a predominantly magnetic dipole character.

It should be noted that these conclusions are specific to the TEM mode. For the 
next higher-order modes, TE\textsubscript{01} and TE\textsubscript{10}, the 
relationship between $\mathbf{E}_n^\pm$ and $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ is more involved 
than \eqref{eqn:he_wave}, and the simple phase-based distinction between electric 
and magnetic dipole character no longer holds.

The individual components of the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ can be 
determined separately by evaluating the output power of the TEM cell. Aligning 
the dipole moment with the $x$- and $y$-directions in turn and determining the output power yields~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		m_{\mathrm{e,x}} = \frac{2\sqrt{P_x}}{E^\pm_{n,x}},
		\label{eqn:e0x_mse}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		m_{\mathrm{e,y}} = \frac{2\sqrt{P_y}}{E^\pm_{n,y}},
		\label{eqn:e0y_mse}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where $P_x$ and $P_y$ denote the output power at one port induced by the 
$x$- and $y$-components of $\mathbf{m}_e$, respectively. Furthermore, $E^\pm_{n,x}$ and $E^\pm_{n,y}$ denote the $x$- and $y$-component of the normalized electric field intensity of mode $n$.

\subsubsection{Electrically small antennas}\label{sec:el_small_antennas}

Electrically small antennas in TEM cells have been effectively 
characterized by equivalent dipole moments in the 
literature~\cites{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024,
	Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}. This representation offers several 
advantages, including insight into the separate electric and magnetic 
coupling mechanisms and a reduced numerical effort compared to full 
antenna simulations. In the following, the coupling behavior of 
electrically small antennas is analyzed and related to equivalent dipole 
moments, following the procedure outlined by 
Collin~\cite[pp.~360--362]{Collin_2015}.

Consider a current $I$ flowing along the curve $C$ of an electrically small thin-wire antenna. The current $I$ is normalized to $\sqrt{\mathrm{W}}$, giving it the unit $\mathrm{A/\sqrt{W}}$, which simplifies both the mathematical analysis and the physical interpretation of the results. Substituting $I$ as the source $\mathbf{J}_1$ integrated over the wire cross-section into \eqref{eqn:an_ej} and \eqref{eqn:bn_ej}, the field coupling to an electrically small antenna is given by~\cite[p.~361]{Collin_2015}

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:e_int_ab}
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-\int_C I(l)\cdot \mathbf{E}_n^+\,d\mathbf{l},
		\label{eqn:e_int_a}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-\int_C I(l)\cdot \mathbf{E}_n^-\,d\mathbf{l}.
		\label{eqn:e_int_b}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

With the aim of establishing a relation between an inductive loop antenna and an 
equivalent magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$, the electrically small antenna is now assumed to form a loop carrying a constant current $I$ over a closed curve $C$. Since $I$ is constant along $C$, it can be pulled out of the integral, and integrating over the closed loop gives~\cite[p.~361]{Collin_2015}

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:e_closed_int}
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-I\oint_C \mathbf{E}_n^+\,d\mathbf{l} = j\omega\mu_0I \iint_S 
		\mathbf{H}_n^+\,d\mathbf{s}'=I\cdot V_n^+,
		\label{eqn:e_a_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-I\oint_C \mathbf{E}_n^-\,d\mathbf{l} = j\omega\mu_0I \iint_S 
		\mathbf{H}_n^-\,d\mathbf{s}'=I\cdot V_n^-.
		\label{eqn:e_b_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

The magnetic field $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ of a given mode $n$ passing the closed loop surface is thus directly related to a normalized induced voltage $V^\pm_n$, which in turn determines the coupling coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$. Applying this to the TEM mode discussed earlier, and substituting the normalized induced voltage $V^\pm_\mathrm{TEM}$ into the expression for $\mathbf{m}_m$ in \eqref{eqn:ifa_mm}, gives

\begin{equation}
	V^+_\mathrm{TEM} - V^-_\mathrm{TEM} = 
	-2j\frac{\left(\mathbf{m}_{m}\times\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z\right)
		\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}{I\,\eta_0}.
	\label{eqn:m_v}
\end{equation}

Therefore, the normalized induced voltage measured at an antenna serves as a direct measure of its equivalent magnetic dipole moment.

In the general case, a magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ producing fields characterized with coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ for any mode $n$ models the magnetic coupling behavior of any electrically small antenna yielding the same coefficients. Consequently, deriving an equivalent magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ of an electrically small antenna is possible by measuring $a_n$ and $b_n$ at the output ports. 

By duality, a similar relation holds for the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$, where the displacement current $I^\pm_n$ of a given mode $n$ between the antenna and the septum serves as its measure, and a constant voltage $V$ at the antenna feedpoint is assumed. For the TEM mode specifically, this duality leads to

\begin{equation}
	I_\mathrm{TEM}^+ + I_\mathrm{TEM}^- = 
	-2\frac{\mathbf{m}_{e}\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}{V}.
	\label{eqn:me_i}
\end{equation}

An equivalent $\mathbf{m}_e$ thus models the electric coupling behavior of any 
electrically small antenna producing the same coefficients, and can 
be determined by measuring these coefficients at the output port, analogous to the 
case of $\mathbf{m}_m$. 

The physical meaning of $I_n$ is the electrical current passing between the septum and the dipole via capacitive coupling, representing the displacement current. In summary, the magnetic dipole moment arises from the induced voltage associated with magnetic coupling, whereas the electric dipole moment results from the coupling displacement current.

These two coupling mechanisms are shown in \autoref{fig:electric_magnetic_coupling}, 
where, for the TEM mode, electric coupling produces in-phase currents at the output 
ports and magnetic coupling produces out-of-phase currents, thus allowing the equivalent electric and magnetic dipole moments of the antenna to be determined separately.

There, the position of the electric and magnetic dipole moments do not matter, as long as the matching electric and magnetic fields at the surfaces are chosen. This is because the field components do not change direction when propagating from the dipoles to the surfaces, due to the symmetrical property of the cylindrical waveguide. This is not the case for a TEM cell. There, an offset into the x- and y-direction from the center leads to field components, which change direction while traveling to the surfaces. Then, the vector product used in the derivation by Lorentz Reciprocity theorem is not valid anymore. Instead, the fields at the test points have to be considered, and because they don't have a singular x,y or z-component anymore, several more dipole moments become relevant.
