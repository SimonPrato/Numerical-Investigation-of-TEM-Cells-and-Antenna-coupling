\subsection{Loop antenna}\label{sec:loop_sim}

In contrast to the monopole antenna, the loop antenna is the most fundamental antenna capable of generating a magnetic dipole moment and is therefore selected as the second case for further analysis.

The output power produced by the equivalent dipole moments is compared to that radiated by the loop antenna in \cref{sec:output_power_loop}, validating the equivalent dipole models. In \cref{sec:geom_dip_loop}, the influence of the loop antenna geometry is examined by varying its height and width, and analyzing the resulting equivalent dipole moments. This parametric study provides additional insight into how the geometrical properties of the loop antenna influence the equivalent dipole moments.

The electric and magnetic coupling mechanisms between the loop antenna and the TEM cell are directly reflected in the frequency-dependent behavior of the feed voltage, feed current, and antenna impedance. As the relationship between these quantities governs the overall coupling behavior, it is analyzed in \cref{sec:loop_electrical_characteristics}. In addition, the current distribution induced on the septum for varying antenna placements and frequencies is investigated in \cref{sec:current_dist_septum_loop}, with particular attention to the influence of the propagating TEM and TE\textsubscript{01} modes on the coupling behavior.

Following the approach established for the monopole antenna, the theoretical framework and numerical results are combined to derive an equivalent circuit model, presented in \cref{sec:eqc_loop}. This equivalent circuit provides deeper insight into the coupling mechanisms between the antenna and the TEM cell, as well as the connection between the generated electric and magnetic dipole moments.

The geometry of the loop antenna model, positioned inside the TEM cell and connected to a feedpoint on the top cell wall, is shown in \autoref{fig:loopantennageometry}. The antenna consists of three wire segments, each of length $h = w = 1.6\,$mm, and the PEC surface as return path, yielding a total circumference of $C = 6.4\,$mm. The antenna remains electrically short ($C < \lambda/10$) for frequencies up to $4.69\,$GHz. A square loop is preferred over a round geometry in the numerical model, as its straight sides allow for a more regular mesh and simplify the interpretation of the resulting equivalent dipole moments, with the mesh additionally refined on the antenna surface and near the feedpoint, following the discussion in \cref{sec:adap_sol_pro}.

The loop antenna is oriented such that the normal vector of its enclosed surface is aligned with the $x$-direction, which maximizes the coupling with the magnetic field component of the TEM mode. Unlike the monopole antenna discussed in \cref{sec:monopole}, the loop antenna provides a current return path. The conducting current induces a rotational electric field, equivalent to a net magnetic field intensity through the loop area, thereby giving rise to a magnetic dipole moment as described by \eqref{eqn:mag_dipole_moment_tem}. Furthermore, displacement currents between the antenna and the septum contribute an electric dipole moment, as expressed in \eqref{eqn:me_i}, analogous to the behavior observed for the monopole antenna. The resulting equivalent electric and magnetic dipole moments are analyzed in detail in \cref{sec:eq_dip_loop}.

\subsubsection{Equivalent dipole moments}\label{sec:eq_dip_loop}

The equivalent dipole moments of the loop antenna are numerically derived by using the numerically computed S-parameters 
(see \cref{sec:s-param-data}) to extract the TEM mode coefficients 
$a_\mathrm{TEM}$, $b_\mathrm{TEM}$ via \eqref{eqn:power_of_poynting}. 
Only the TEM mode is considered, since the investigated frequency range 
lies below the cut-off frequency of the first higher-order mode. The dipole 
moments are then computed by applying \eqref{eqn:ifa_me} and 
\eqref{eqn:ifa_mm} with these coefficients. The obtained dipole moments are presented as a function of frequency in \autoref{fig:dipole_moments_loop_antenna}. The phases of the output port powers at ports 1 and 2 are shown in \autoref{fig:loopphase}. Unlike the monopole antenna case, where the phase shift remained zero across the entire investigated frequency range, this phase information is essential for the decomposition of the equivalent dipole moments. A phase shift of zero between the output port powers indicates the presence of an electric dipole moment, whereas a phase shift of $\pm\pi$ is characteristic of a magnetic dipole moment. A phase shift between $0$ and $\pi$ indicates a superposition of both dipole moment contributions, as discussed in detail in \cref{sec:equ-dip-mom}.

The phase shift approaches $\pi$ at low frequencies, indicating that the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ dominates over the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$. However, the phase shift decreases with frequency, giving raise to an electric dipole moment as a consequence. In contrast to the monopole antenna, $\mathbf{m}_e$ and $\mathbf{m}_m$ thereby demonstrate non-linear behavior over frequency.

\subsubsection{Investigation of Field Regions}\label{sec:field_regions}

The non-linear frequency behavior of $\mathbf{m}_m$ observed in 
\autoref{fig:dipole_moments_loop_antenna} raises the question of whether 
this behavior is intrinsic to the antenna geometry or whether it is instead 
caused by transitions between the near-field, intermediate-field, and 
far-field regions within the TEM cell, as introduced in 
\cref{sec:rad_fields}. As the physical distance $r$ between the source and 
the septum is fixed by the cell geometry, the dimensionless product $k \cdot 
r$ increases with frequency, potentially shifting the coupling from the 
near-field into the intermediate- or far-field regime across the investigated 
frequency range. If such a field-region transition contributes to the 
coupling behavior, a change in cell size, which alters $r$ while keeping 
the source fixed, should produce a noticeable change in the 
normalized output power.

To isolate these effects, a numerical study was conducted using two TEM cells 
of significantly different sizes, containing an electric and a magnetic dipole moment, as shown in \autoref{fig:krtemcell}. A 
large cell ($a=40\,\mathrm{mm}$, $b=24\,\mathrm{mm}$) and a small cell 
($a=10\,\mathrm{mm}$, $b=6\,\mathrm{mm}$) are compared, both designed to 
maintain a characteristic impedance of $Z_w \approx 50\,\Omega$. Scaling 
the cell geometry while preserving the impedance changes the physical 
distance $r$ between the source and the septum, thereby shifting the 
$k \cdot r$ factor across the investigated frequency range. Electric and magnetic dipole moments with the 
frequency-dependent magnitudes shown in \autoref{fig:linearmomentws} are 
placed at the center of both cells.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{content/img/kr_tem_cell}
	\caption{Numerical model of a TEM cell containing an electric dipole 
		moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ and a magnetic dipole moment 
		$\mathbf{m}_m$ positioned at the center 
		($x=0,\,y=r=b/4,\,z=0$). This setup is used to investigate whether 
		the dominant coupling field region influences the output power.}
	\label{fig:krtemcell}
\end{figure}

The resulting $k \cdot r$ factor for both TEM cells is shown in 
\autoref{fig:krfactor}, where $r = b/4 = 6\,\mathrm{mm}$ for the large 
cell and $r = b/4 = 1.5\,\mathrm{mm}$ for the small cell, corresponding 
to the distance from the dipole source at the cell center to the septum. 
Across the entire investigated frequency range, $k \cdot r$ remains well 
below unity for both configurations, confirming that the coupling operates within the near-field regime throughout.

\begin{figure}[htbp]

The resulting output powers, normalized to unity for direct comparison, are 
presented together with the input dipole moments in 
\autoref{fig:linear_moments_power}. The output power of the small and large 
cells are in close agreement across the entire frequency range, demonstrating 
that the varying $k\cdot r$ product has a negligible influence on the frequency-dependence of the output power. The non-linear behavior of the normalized output power 
is therefore not a consequence of field-region transitions within the TEM 
cell, but is instead governed exclusively by the frequency dependence of the 
source dipole moments themselves.

\subsubsection{Output power}\label{sec:output_power_loop}

The output power and the electric field intensity $E^\pm_y$ at $x=0$, $y=b/4$, $z=\pm l/2$ induced by the loop antenna at the output ports are shown in \autoref{fig:loopopower}. Compared to the monopole antenna results presented in \autoref{fig:monopoleoutputpower}, both quantities exhibit a less steep increase with frequency. This behavior is directly attributed to the frequency dependence of the magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$: while $\mathbf{m}_m \propto f$ at low frequencies, its growth transitions toward $\mathbf{m}_m \propto \sqrt{f}$ at higher frequencies. Since the output power scales quadratically with the dipole moment amplitude, this reduced growth leads to a lower rate of power increase with frequency. The increase in $\mathbf{m}_e$ does not compensate for this power reduction, as its influence on the output power is small. The investigation demonstrates that the increasing capacitive coupling at higher frequencies enhances the electric dipole moment contribution and partially opposes the magnetic dipole moment, thereby reducing the overall power transfer.

\autoref{fig:loopopowercomp} demonstrates the output power generated by the equivalent dipole moments $\mathbf{m}_m$, $\mathbf{m}_e$ and the loop antenna, derived with \eqref{eqn:power_antenna} using S-parameters. 
The values agree within $\pm10\,$µW, thereby confirming the validity of the dipole moment model.

The current $I$ in the loop antenna is evaluated at a distance of $0.17\,$mm above the feedpoint and at the same height above the PEC plane along the return path, as indicated in \autoref{fig:loopantennageometry}. This offset from the feedpoint and the PEC plane reduces numerical artifacts arising from the large displacement currents in their vicinity, thereby improving the accuracy of the extracted current values. The current $I$ is obtained by integrating the magnetic field intensity $\mathbf{H}$ along a closed curve $C$ of radius $0.11\,$mm around the respective wire segments, according to \eqref{eqn:ampere_law_fem}. 

The extracted current values differ between the two paths, which indicates that displacement currents couple from the antenna to the septum, giving rise to the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ according to \eqref{eqn:me_i}, and return to the feedpoint, constituting the reactive electric energy stored by the antenna. Furthermore, this difference increases with frequency, consistent with the growing displacement currents and the associated increase in $\mathbf{m}_e$. This observation is in good agreement with $\mathbf{m}_e$ presented in \autoref{fig:dipole_moments_loop_antenna}.

\autoref{fig:loopfeedreturncurrent} shows the voltage at the feedpoint of the antenna. A fraction of the antenna voltage contributes to the magnetic coupling with the septum, giving rise to $\mathbf{m}_m$ according to \eqref{eqn:m_v}, while the remainder constitutes the magnetic energy stored in the antenna. The feed voltage significantly rises with frequency and flattens at higher frequencies, which strongly correlates with the frequency-behavior of $\mathbf{m}_m$ shown in \autoref{fig:dipole_moments_loop_antenna}. This correlation validates the theoretical framework established describing the magnetic dipole moment creation of antennas in this thesis.

Lastly, the increases in voltage and decrease in current agrees with the impedance, depicted in \autoref{fig:currimp}. The loop antenna also shows strongly inductive behavior, as expected by the pre-dominantly magnetic energy stored, magnetic dipole moment and inductive geometry. 

It is further noted that the increasing voltage mirrors an increased potential and charge accumulation on the loop antenna, thereby increasing the displacement currents coupling to the septum. Therefore, the generation of a magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ in the loop antenna is naturally accompanied by a electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$. Lastly, the increase in feed voltage and decrease in feed current are consistent with the antenna input impedance shown in \autoref{fig:currimp}. Furthermore, it exhibits strongly inductive behavior, as expected given the predominantly magnetic energy storage, the dominant magnetic dipole moment, and the inherently inductive geometry of the loop antenna.

\subsubsection{Current distribution on septum}\label{sec:current_dist_septum_loop}

The loop antenna induces surface currents on the septum of the TEM cell, as shown in \autoref{fig:loop_surface_current}. The spatial distribution and magnitude of these currents depend on the antenna position and orientation, the excitation frequency, and the propagating modes supported by the cell. An analysis of the induced surface currents therefore provides direct insight into the coupling mechanisms governing the power transfer to the output ports.

When the loop antenna is placed centrally without rotation, thereby placed in the $yz$-plane as shown in \autoref{fig:loopantennageometry}, and operating at $f=3\,\mathrm{GHz}$, its magnetic dipole moment predominantly excites the TEM mode of the cell. \autoref{fig:current_loop_surface_current} shows that the surface currents reaching the two output ports are out of phase. This behavior is consistent with the analysis in \cref{sec:equ-dip-mom}, which predicts a phase difference of $\pm\pi$ between the output port powers for magnetic dipole excitation.

When the antenna is rotated by $\pm\pi/4$ around the $y$-axis and laterally offset by $x=7\,\mathrm{mm}$, the transmitted power at $f=3\,\mathrm{GHz}$ becomes negligible. According to \eqref{eqn:e_a_closed_int} and \eqref{eqn:e_b_closed_int}, efficient magnetic coupling requires alignment between the magnetic field of the propagating mode, $\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$, and the surface normal vector of the loop antenna. In this configuration, the normal vector of the loop surface is oriented along the $z$-direction, whereas $\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$ is primarily directed along the $x$-axis. As a result, the overlap integral governing magnetic coupling is strongly reduced. The current distribution shown in \autoref{fig:loopsurfacecurrentsoffsetrotation} confirms that no significant traveling waves are excited. Instead, the induced currents form predominantly closed circulation patterns and remain largely reactive, such that only a negligible amount of current reaches the output ports.

At $f=3.3\,\mathrm{GHz}$, the TE\textsubscript{01} mode propagates in addition to the TEM mode. In this case, the magnetic field component $\mathbf{h}_\mathrm{TE01}^\pm$ exhibits a significant $z$-component near the cell walls, which aligns with the normal vector of the rotated loop surface. This alignment substantially enhances magnetic coupling. As shown in \autoref{fig:loopsurfacecurrentsoffsetrotation3ghz3}, a considerable portion of the induced surface current now propagates toward the output ports, resulting in measurable power transmission. In contrast to the purely TEM case, the output powers are in phase, as discussed in \cref{sec:equ-dip-mom}.

The frequency-dependent power transfer for this configuration is shown in \autoref{fig:loopsurfacecurrentsoffsetrotation3ghz3plot}, where the contributions of the TEM and TE\textsubscript{01} modes are presented separately. At $f=3.21\,\mathrm{GHz}$, a resonance occurs within the TEM cell, resulting in a pronounced peak in the output power associated with the TE\textsubscript{01} mode. This resonance corresponds to a condition in which half the wavelength matches the effective electrical length of the TEM cell, as previously discussed in \cref{sec:higher-order-modes}.

Notably, the power transmitted via the TE\textsubscript{01} mode exceeds that of the TEM mode and remains significant even below its theoretical cut-off frequency of $f_{\mathrm{c,01}}=3.12\,\mathrm{GHz}$. This behavior illustrates the theoretical framework established in \cref{sec:el_small_antennas}, which describes the magnetic coupling of electrically small antennas in terms of modal field alignment.

To determine $R_A$, $L_A$ and $C_A$, the antenna model is placed on a PEC 
surface in an open space, as shown in \autoref{fig:free-space-loop}. 
The inductance and capacitance are derived according to 
\eqref{eqn:l_m_energy} and \eqref{eqn:c_e_energy}, which leads to

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		L_A = 2\frac{W_m}{I_{LA}^2},
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		C_A = 2\frac{W_c}{V_{CA}^2},
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where $I_{LA}$ denotes the current through the inductor 
$L_A$ and $V_{CA}$ denotes the voltage across the capacitor 
$C_A$. The resulting capacitance and inductance equal $C_A=38.2\,\mathrm{fF}$ 
and $L_A=2.14\,\mathrm{nH}$.

The equivalent dipole moments are extracted from the circuit following 
the same two-step procedure described in \cref{sec:monopole_eqc}: first, 
the induced voltage across $L_{T1}$ and $L_{T2}$ and the displacement 
current through $C_k$ are identified as the source quantities for 
$\mathbf{m}_m$ and $\mathbf{m}_e$, respectively. Then, Kirchhoff's laws 
are applied to determine the fraction of each quantity reaching the 
termination resistances $R_1$ and $R_2$. Unlike the monopole antenna, 
both the electric and the magnetic coupling mechanisms are active here, as the loop antenna exhibits a significant magnetic dipole moment in addition to the electric one.

The antenna equivalent circuit is extended with the TEM cell coupling model 
introduced in \cref{sec:tem_cell_model}, as shown in \autoref{fig:full_circuit_loop}. The coupling components $C_k$, $M_{A,T1}$ and $M_{A,T2}$ are defined analogously to the equivalent circuit of the monopole antenna in \cref{sec:monopole_eqc}.
