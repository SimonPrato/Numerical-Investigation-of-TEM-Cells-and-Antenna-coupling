{\Large
\noindent
{\bf Abstract}} \\
\vspace*{0.3 cm}

Electrically small antennas conducting high-frequency signals can produce large levels of electromagnetic emissions. The characterization of these emissions by measurement in a TEM cell remains a widely used procedure. This thesis aims to provide a theoretical framework for the mechanics behind the coupling between the antenna and the TEM cell, and investigate these concepts with numerical analysis.

In context of these investigations, an emphasize is laid on electric and magnetic dipole moments, which effectively represent electrically small radiating sources. The magnitudes of those dipole moments accurately reflect the electric and magnetic coupling separately. Although methods for determining these magnitudes by measurements with the TEM cell are available, this thesis focuses on leveraging the finite element method, to eliminate the possible presence of inaccuracies caused by the measurement setup and procedure.  

The findings in this thesis assist in understanding the geometrical characteristics of the antennas, which are the driving forces behind the coupling between the electrically small antennas and TEM cell, and provide guidelines for the design of such antennas with a specified coupling behavior. This proofs useful, for example, when aiming to achieve increased electromagnetic compatibility of an electronic system containing electrically small conducting structures.
