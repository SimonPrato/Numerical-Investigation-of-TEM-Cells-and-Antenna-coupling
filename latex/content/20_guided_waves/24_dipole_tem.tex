\subsection{Radiating Sources in TEM Cells}\label{sec:rad_source_tem}

\subsubsection{Arbitrary source}

Consider a current $\mathbf{J}_1$ exciting the TEM cell, as shown in 
\autoref{fig:reciprocitytemcell}. In general, such a current requires external fields to sustain it. However, since the purpose of this analysis is to determine the fields radiated by $\mathbf{J}_1$, these external fields are excluded, following the analytical setup of~\cite[p.~360]{Collin_2015}. Only the radiated fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ are considered, which satisfy Maxwell's equations~\cite[p.~360]{Collin_2015}:

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		\nabla \times \mathbf{E}=-j\omega\mu_0\mathbf{H},
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\nabla\times\mathbf{H}=j\omega\epsilon_0\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{J}_1.
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}


\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{content/img/reciprocity_tem_cell}
	\caption{TEM cell with an arbitrary current source $\mathbf{J}_1$ along the line $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ \cite{Collin_2015}. $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ are the field intensities induced by the current. $\mathbf{e}^+_n$ and $\mathbf{e}^-_n$ are outgoing fields towards both output ports of the TEM cell. $S_1$ and $S_2$ denote the surfaces at the output ports, and $d\mathbf{s}_1'$, $d\mathbf{s}_2'$ the corresponding outward-directed vector surface elements.}
	\label{fig:reciprocitytemcell}
\end{figure}

Applying the integral form of the Lorentz reciprocity theorem in \eqref{eqn:lorentz_rec_theorem_int}, where its other source terms are neglected $\mathbf{J}_2=\mathbf{M}_1=\mathbf{M}_2=0$, yields

\begin{equation}
	\oiint _S (\mathbf{E}_n^\pm\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^\pm)\,d\mathbf{s'}=\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm\,dv'.
	\label{eqn:J1_propagating_waves}
\end{equation}

The integration is carried out over the volume $V$ enclosed by the TEM cell walls, the septum, and the output port surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$. The Lorentz reciprocity theorem therefore establishes a relation between the fields $\mathbf{E}$, $\mathbf{H}$ generated by the source $\mathbf{J}_1$ and the fields $\mathbf{E}_n^\pm$, $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ of an arbitrary mode $n$, which will be used extensively throughout this thesis. Since the tangential fields vanish at the perfectly conducting walls and septum, the only non-zero contributions arise from the output port surfaces, yielding

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		\iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times 
		\mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' = 
		\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+\,dv',
		\label{eqn:s1_lorentz}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		\iint_{S_2} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^-\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times 
		\mathbf{H}_n^-\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' = 
		\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-\,dv'.
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

The following analysis focuses on the surface $S_1$ and its associated integral 
equation~\eqref{eqn:s1_lorentz}, as the treatment of $S_2$ follows analogously. 
Substituting the modal expansions \eqref{eqn:a_modal_superposition1}--\eqref{eqn:b_modal_superposition2} 
for the fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{H}$ radiated by $\mathbf{J}_1$ into the 
left-hand side yields

\begin{align}
	\iint_{S_1} &\left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)
	\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \sum_m a_m\mathbf{H}_m^+
	-\sum_m a_m\mathbf{E}_m^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \sum_m a_m \iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_m^+
	-\mathbf{E}_m^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}'.
\end{align}

Applying the orthogonality condition~\eqref{eqn:norm_power} and the normalization 
condition~\eqref{eqn:unit_power}, the coefficient $a_n$ of mode $n$ can be isolated as

\begin{align}
	\iint_{S_1} \left(\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}
	-\mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}'
	&= a_n \iint_{S_1} \left(-\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+
	-\mathbf{E}_n^+\times \mathbf{H}_n^+\right)\,d\mathbf{s}' 
	= -2a_n,
\end{align}
leading to
\begin{equation}
	a_n = -\frac{1}{2}\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+\,dv',
\end{equation}
and analogously
\begin{equation}
	b_n = -\frac{1}{2}\iiint_V \mathbf{J}_1\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-\,dv'.
\end{equation}
This result is significant, as it directly relates an arbitrary source $\mathbf{J}_1$ to the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ of every propagating mode in the TEM cell. Consequently, knowledge of the normalized field distributions of the relevant modes over a frequency range of interest is sufficient to fully characterize the output fields generated by any internal source.

Furthermore, the output power at each port can be directly expressed in terms of the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$. For the TEM mode, this takes a particularly simple 
form, hence it is presented here as an example. Combining the normalization condition~\eqref{eqn:unit_power} with the modal fields 
$\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$ and $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm$ and their respective 
coefficients $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $b_\mathrm{TEM}$ yields
\begin{subequations}
	\begin{align}
		P_{\mathrm{out,1}} &= \iint_{S_1} \mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}\,d\mathbf{s}'\nonumber\\ 
		&= \iint_{S_1} \frac{1}{2}\,\Re\left\{ 
		\left(a_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^+\right) \times 
		\left(a_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^+\right)^* 
		\right\}\,d\mathbf{s}'= \frac{|a_\mathrm{TEM}|^2}{2},
		\label{eqn:power_of_poynting1}
	\end{align}
	\begin{align}
		P_{\mathrm{out,2}} &= \iint_{S_2} \mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
		&= \iint_{S_2} \frac{1}{2}\,\Re\left\{ 
		\left(b_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^-\right) \times 
		\left(b_\mathrm{TEM}\,\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^-\right)^* 
		\right\}\,d\mathbf{s}' = \frac{|b_\mathrm{TEM}|^2}{2},
		\label{eqn:power_of_poynting2}
	\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where the time-averaged Poynting vector $\mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av}$ of the TEM mode is 
purely real, such that
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{S}_\mathrm{av} 
	= \frac{1}{2}\Re\left\{\mathbf{E}
	\times\mathbf{H}^*\right\}
	= \frac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{H}.
	\label{eqn:equivalent_tem}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Equivalent dipole moments}\label{sec:equ-dip-mom}

Since this thesis focuses, among other things, on the analysis of dipole moment sources 
in the TEM cell, the previously discussed arbitrary source formulation is now specialized to the dipole moment case. An 
electric dipole moment is defined through a current flowing along an infinitesimally 
short wire, $\mathbf{m}_e = I_0 d$, as in \cref{eqn:m_e_def}. Substituting this as 
the source $\mathbf{J}_1$ into the integral formulation of the Lorentz reciprocity 
theorem~\eqref{eqn:J1_propagating_waves} yields~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:an_bn_me}
	\begin{equation}
		a_n = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^+,
		\label{eqn:an_me}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		b_n = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^-.
		\label{eqn:bn_me}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

These two expressions are compactly written as

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm,
	\label{eqn:dipole_tem_waves}
\end{equation}

where the upper and lower entries correspond to the `$+$' and `$-$' cases 
of \eqref{eqn:an_me} and \eqref{eqn:bn_me}, respectively.

The general source formulation is now specialized to the case of a magnetic dipole 
moment $\mathbf{M}_m$, following the approach of Collin~\cite[p.~361]{Collin_2015}. 
The source current $\mathbf{J}_1$ is modeled as a constant current $I_0$ flowing 
around an infinitesimally small loop along a curve $C$, which gives

\begin{align}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} 
	&= -\frac{I_0}{2}\oint_C \mathbf{E}_n^\pm \,d\mathbf{l} \nonumber \\
	&= -\frac{I_0}{2}\iint_{S} \nabla \times \mathbf{E}_n^\pm 
	\,d\mathbf{s}' \nonumber\\
	&= \frac{j\omega\mu_0 I_0}{2}\iint_{S} \mathbf{H}_n^\pm 
	\,d\mathbf{s}'.
	\label{eqn:mag_dipole_moment_tem}
\end{align}

The surface integral is thus expressed in terms of the normalized magnetic field 
distribution $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ of mode $n$. Since the loop is infinitesimally small, 
$\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ is assumed constant over the loop area, allowing the source to be 
represented by $\mathbf{M}_m = I_0\,\mathbf{A}$ as defined in~\eqref{eqn:M_m}. 
Substituting this definition into~\eqref{eqn:mag_dipole_moment_tem} yields

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} 
	= \frac{j\omega\mu_0}{2}\,\mathbf{M}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm.
\end{equation}

As discussed in \cref{sec:mag_dip}, the magnetic dipole moment is conventionally 
expressed in numerical codes and in the 
literature via a 
magnetic current flowing along a given length~\cite{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024}, defined as $\mathbf{m}_m = I_m\mathbf{L}$ 
in \eqref{eqn:mag_dipole_moment}. Inserting the equivalence 
$\mathbf{m}_m = \mathbf{M}_m\mu_0\omega$~\cite{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024} 
yields

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = \frac{j}{2}\,\mathbf{m}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm.
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments_mag}
\end{equation}

In the presence of both an electric and a magnetic dipole moment, their contributions superpose, giving \cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_n \\ b_n\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}
	\left(-\mathbf{m}_e\cdot\mathbf{E}_n^\pm 
	+ j\,\mathbf{m}_m\cdot\mathbf{H}_n^\pm\right).
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments}
\end{equation}

This expression relates the dipole moments $\mathbf{m}_e$ and $\mathbf{m}_m$ directly 
to the coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ of any propagating mode $n$. Since the TEM mode 
is of large interest in this thesis, \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments} is now specialized 
using the relation between the normalized TEM mode fields~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981},

\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm = \pm\,\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z\times\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm / \eta_0.
	\label{eqn:he_wave}
\end{equation}

Substituting this into \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments} yields~\cite{Kreindl_Bauernfeind_Weiss_Stockreiter_Kaltenbacher_2024}

\begin{equation}
	\begin{pmatrix}a_\mathrm{TEM} \\ b_\mathrm{TEM}\end{pmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}\,\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm\cdot
	\left(\mathbf{m}_e 
	\pm j\frac{\mathbf{m}_m\times\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z}{\eta_0}\right),
	\label{eqn:a_b_moments_tem}
\end{equation}

where the $\pm$ sign on the magnetic term reflects the anti-symmetry of the TEM 
magnetic field at the two output ports, $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^+ = 
-\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^-$, as shown in \autoref{fig:reciprocitytemcelltemmode}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{content/img/reciprocity_tem_cell_tem_mode}
	\caption{Field distribution of the TEM mode, highlighting the anti-phase magnetic 
		fields at the two output ports.}
	\label{fig:reciprocitytemcelltemmode}
\end{figure}

Since \eqref{eqn:a_b_moments_tem} requires knowledge of only $\mathbf{E}^\pm_\mathrm{TEM}$, 
it is particularly convenient for determining the dipole moments from measured or 
simulated port data. The individual contributions of $\mathbf{m}_e$ and $\mathbf{m}_m$ 
can be separated by forming the sum and difference of $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and 
$b_\mathrm{TEM}$~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981},

\begin{subequations}\label{eqn:abm}
	\begin{equation}
		a_\mathrm{TEM} + b_\mathrm{TEM} = 
		-\mathbf{m}_{e}\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm,
		\label{eqn:ifa_me}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		a_\mathrm{TEM} - b_\mathrm{TEM} = 
		-j\frac{\left(\mathbf{m}_{m}\times\hat{\mathbf{a}}_z\right)
			\cdot\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}{\eta_0},
		\label{eqn:ifa_mm}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where the dipole moments are assumed to be located at the midpoint ($z=0$) between the two 
output ports. A displacement 
along the $z$-direction would introduce a phase shift between 
$\mathbf{E}^+_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $\mathbf{E}^-_\mathrm{TEM}$, which would invalidate 
the separation in \eqref{eqn:abm}.

The expressions in \eqref{eqn:abm} yield useful physical insight into how electric and magnetic dipole moments couple to the TEM cell. Consider first a pure electric dipole moment, $\mathbf{m}_m = 0$. The right-hand side of \eqref{eqn:ifa_mm} vanishes, giving $a_\mathrm{TEM} = b_\mathrm{TEM}$: the two output port signals are identical and therefore in phase. Conversely, for a pure magnetic dipole moment, $\mathbf{m}_e = 0$, the right-hand side of \eqref{eqn:ifa_me} vanishes, giving $a_\mathrm{TEM} = -b_\mathrm{TEM}$: the two output port signals are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign, corresponding to a phase shift of $\pi$. In general, the phase relationship between $a_\mathrm{TEM}$ and $b_\mathrm{TEM}$ therefore directly identifies the nature of the dominant source. In-phase outputs indicate a predominantly electric dipole character, while out-of-phase outputs indicate a predominantly magnetic dipole character.

%For the TEM mode, an electric dipole placed in the TEM cell leads to output power with the same phase at both ports. This directly results from the symmetric field distribution of the TEM mode leading to the same $\mathbf{E}^\pm_\mathrm{TEM}$ component at the output ports. In contrast, a magnetic dipole results in a phase shift of $\pm\pi$. This difference arises from the phase shift of the magnetic fields at the output ports, $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^+$ and $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TEM}^-$, as illustrate in \autoref{fig:reciprocitytemcelltemmode}. 

It should be noted that these conclusions are specific to the TEM mode. For the 
next higher-order modes, TE\textsubscript{01} and TE\textsubscript{10}, the 
relationship between $\mathbf{E}_n^\pm$ and $\mathbf{H}_n^\pm$ is more involved 
than \eqref{eqn:he_wave}, and the simple phase-based distinction between electric 
and magnetic dipole character no longer holds.

%An electric dipole moment leads to a phase shift of $\pi$, while a magnetic dipole moment produces no phase shift. This behavior is due to the asymmetric field distribution of the TE\textsubscript{01} mode, thus a phase shift occurs now between the magnetic field intensities at the output ports, $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TE01}^+$ and $\mathbf{H}_\mathrm{TE01}^-$, while there is again no phase shift between the electric field intensities, $\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TE01}^+$ and $\mathbf{E}_\mathrm{TE01}^-$.

The individual components of the electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ can be 
determined separately by evaluating the output power of the TEM cell. Aligning 
the dipole moment with the $x$- and $y$-directions in turn and applying 
\eqref{eqn:ifa_me} yields~\cite{Sreenivasiah_Chang_Ma_1981}

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		m_{\mathrm{e,x}} = \frac{2\sqrt{P_x}}{E^\pm_{n,x}},
		\label{eqn:e0x_mse}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		m_{\mathrm{e,y}} = \frac{2\sqrt{P_y}}{E^\pm_{n,y}},
		\label{eqn:e0y_mse}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

where $P_x$ and $P_y$ denote the output power at one port induced by the 
$x$- and $y$-components of $\mathbf{m}_e$, respectively.



\subsubsection{Electrically small antennas}\label{sec:el_small_antennas}

The electric field coupling with an electrically small antenna can be expressed as \cite[p. 361]{Collin_2015}

\begin{subequations}
	
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-\int_C \boldsymbol{\tau} I(l)\cdot \mathbf{e}_n^+\,dl.
		\label{eqn:e_int_a}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-\int_C \boldsymbol{\tau} I(l)\cdot \mathbf{e}_n^-\,dl.
		\label{eqn:e_int_b}
	\end{equation}
	
\end{subequations}

Since the antenna is electrically small, the electric field $\mathbf{e}_n^\pm$ is assumed to be constant in $C$. Furthermore, if the current $I$ is constant along $C$, it does not have to be considered in the integration. Integrating over the closed loop simplifies to \cite[p. 361]{Collin_2015}

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-\oint_C \mathbf{e}_n^+\cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\,dl = j\omega\mu_0 \iint_S \mathbf{h}_n^+\,d\mathbf{s}'=V_n^+,
		\label{eqn:e_a_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-\oint_C \mathbf{e}_n^-\cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\,dl = j\omega\mu_0 \iint_S \mathbf{h}_n^-\,d\mathbf{s}'=V_n^-.
		\label{eqn:e_b_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

The induced voltage $V_n^+$ is related to the fields $\mathbf{e}_n^+$ and $\mathbf{h}_n^+$ at the output port located in positive $z$-direction, while the induced voltage $V_n^-$ is related to the fields at the other output port $\mathbf{e}_n^-$ and $\mathbf{h}_n^-$. For the TEM mode, the total induced voltage equals $V_\mathrm{TEM}=V_\mathrm{TEM}^--V_\mathrm{TEM}^+$. The relation to the magnitude of the magnetic dipole moment ${m}_m$ is expressed as

\begin{equation}
	m_m=\frac{a_\mathrm{TEM}-b_\mathrm{TEM}}{e_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm\cdot k_0}=\frac{V_\mathrm{TEM}}{{e}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm\cdot k_0}.
	\label{eqn:m_v}
\end{equation}

For the TE\textsubscript{01} mode, the total induced voltage equals $V_\mathrm{TE01}=V_\mathrm{TE01}^-+V_\mathrm{TE01}^+$. 

In the general case, a magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ producing fields characterized with coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ models the magnetic coupling behavior of any electrically small antenna yielding the same coefficients. Consequently, deriving an equivalent magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_m$ of an electrically small antenna is possible by measuring $a_n$ and $b_n$ at the output ports. 

In a similar manner to \eqref{eqn:e_int_a} and \eqref{eqn:e_int_b}, a constant magnetic field $\mathbf{h}_n^\pm$ along a magnetic current $I_m$ following a curve $C$ makes the following expression possible:

\begin{subequations}
	
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-\int_C \boldsymbol{\tau} I_m(l)\cdot \mathbf{h}_n^+\,dl,
		\label{eqn:h_int_a}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-\int_C \boldsymbol{\tau} I_m(l)\cdot \mathbf{h}_n^-\,dl.
		\label{eqn:h_int_b}
	\end{equation}
	
\end{subequations}

Analogous to \eqref{eqn:e_a_closed_int} and \eqref{eqn:e_b_closed_int}, $I_m$ is assumed to be constant and $C$ to form a closed loop, leading to 

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n=-\oint_C \mathbf{h}_n^- \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\,dl = -j\omega\epsilon_0 \iint_{S_0} \mathbf{e}_n^+\,d\mathbf{s}',
		\label{eqn:h_a_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n=-\oint_C \mathbf{h}_n^+ \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\,dl = -j\omega\epsilon_0 \iint_{S_0} \mathbf{e}_n^-\,d\mathbf{s}'.
		\label{eqn:h_b_closed_int}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

Now, the surfaces $S_1$ and $S_2$ are defined, which are depicted in \autoref{fig:displacement-current-tem-cell}. Surface $S_1$ starts from $S_0$ and runs parallel to the electric field $\mathbf{e}_n^\pm$, extending infinitely. A total surface is defined as $S=S_0+S_1+S_2$, where $S_2$ closes the total surface around $S_1$ at infinity. 

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{content/img/displacement-current-tem-cell}
	\caption{Sketch of the surfaces $S_0, S_1$ and $S_2$ in the TEM cell with an example antenna, coupling through $S_0$ to the septum over the displacement current $\partial \mathbf{D}/\partial t$.}
	\label{fig:displacement-current-tem-cell}
\end{figure}

Consequently, \eqref{eqn:h_a_closed_int} and \eqref{eqn:h_b_closed_int} can be written with the newly defined closed surface $S$ as 


\begin{equation}
	j \omega \epsilon_0 \oiint_{S} \mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot d\mathbf{s'}=j \omega \epsilon_0 \iint_{S_0} \mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot d\mathbf{s'}+\underbrace{j \omega \epsilon_0 \iint_{S_1} \mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot d\mathbf{s'}}_{=0}+\underbrace{j \omega \epsilon_0 \iint_{S_2} \mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot d\mathbf{S}}_{=0}.
\end{equation}

Inserting Gauss' law $\nabla\cdot \mathbf{E}=\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$ leads to

\begin{equation}
	-j \omega \epsilon_0 \oiint_{S} \mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot d\mathbf{s'} = -j \omega \epsilon_0 \iiint_{V} \nabla \cdot\mathbf{e}_n^{\pm} \cdot dv' = -j \omega \iiint_{V} {\rho}_n^\pm \cdot dv'.
\end{equation}

With the continuity equation $j\omega\rho = -\nabla\cdot \mathbf{J}$ this yields

\begin{subequations}
	\begin{equation}
		2a_n = -j \omega \iiint_{V} {\rho}_n^+ \cdot dv' = \iiint_{V} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_n^+ \cdot dv' = \oiint_{S} \mathbf{J}_n^+ \cdot d\mathbf{s'} =I_n^+,
		\label{eqn:charges_a}
	\end{equation}
	\begin{equation}
		2b_n = -j \omega \iiint_{V} {\rho}_n^- \cdot dv' = \iiint_{V} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_n^- \cdot dv' = \oiint_{S} \mathbf{J}_n^- \cdot d\mathbf{s'} = I_n^-.
		\label{eqn:charges_b}
	\end{equation}
\end{subequations}

For the TEM mode, the electric dipole moment magnitude ${m}_e$ can be expressed in terms of the total current $I_\mathrm{TEM}=I_\mathrm{TEM}^+ + I_\mathrm{TEM}^-$ by substituting \eqref{eqn:charges_a} and \eqref{eqn:charges_b} into \eqref{eqn:ifa_me}, yielding
\begin{equation}
	{m}_e=\frac{a_\mathrm{TEM}+b_\mathrm{TEM}}{{e}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}=\frac{I_\mathrm{TEM}}{{e}_\mathrm{TEM}^\pm}.
	\label{eqn:me_i}
\end{equation}

An electric dipole moment $\mathbf{m}_e$ producing fields characterized with coefficients $a_n$ and $b_n$ models the electric coupling behavior of any electrically small antenna yielding the same coefficients. Consequently, deriving an equivalent $\mathbf{m}_e$ of an electrically small antenna is possible by measuring $a_n$ and $b_n$ at the output port, analogous to the case of $\mathbf{m}_m$. 

The physical meaning of $I_n$ is the electrical current passing between the septum and the dipole via capacitive coupling, representing the displacement current. In summary, the magnetic dipole moment arises from the induced voltage on the septum, whereas the electric dipole moment results from the coupling displacement current.

There, the position of the electric and magnetic dipole moments do not matter, as long as the matching electric and magnetic fields at the surfaces are chosen. This is because the field components do not change direction when propagating from the dipoles to the surfaces, due to the symmetrical property of the cylindrical waveguide. Then, the vector product used in the derivation by Lorentz Reciprocity theorem is not valid anymore.
